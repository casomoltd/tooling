# Casomo Writing Style

This skill defines the Casomo writing voice. Read and internalise these patterns before writing anything that will carry the brand or the founder's name.

## Core Voice

The voice reads like an engineer explaining something clearly to someone they respect. Direct, warm but not performative, grounded in specifics rather than abstractions. It sounds like a person, not a brand.

**The voice is:** confident without being cocky, honest without being self-deprecating, opinionated without being preachy.

**The voice is NOT:** corporate, salesy, breathless, hedging, or trying to impress.

## Sentence Structure

- Favours short-to-medium sentences. Lets important statements land on their own.
- Uses fragments deliberately for emphasis: "Not approximate rigour. Not rigour when convenient."
- Builds rhythm through parallel structure and repetition: "More tools, more layers, more process — but not always more usefulness."
- Often follows a general observation with a grounded, specific restatement: sets up a contrast, then lands it.
- Sentences tend to end on the strongest word or idea, not trail off.

## Paragraph Structure

- Paragraphs are typically 2–4 sentences. Rarely longer.
- Single-sentence paragraphs used for emphasis: "That contrast stuck with me."
- Often structures an argument as: observation → tension → resolution. Or: common assumption → why it's wrong → what actually matters.
- Transitions between paragraphs are implicit. Avoids connective tissue like "Furthermore," "Additionally," "In conclusion." The logic carries the reader forward.

## Punctuation and Formatting

- Heavy use of em dashes for asides and pivots — not parentheses, not semicolons.
- Sparing with bold. Never uses it for emphasis mid-sentence. Uses it only for structural labels or key phrases that anchor a section.
- No exclamation marks in professional copy.
- Minimal use of commas — only where needed for clarity. Prefers shorter sentences over long ones broken up by commas.
- No Oxford comma (follows UK convention).

## Word Choice

- Plain, concrete language. "Build" not "architect." "Works" not "delivers value." "Prove" not "validate."
- Technical terms used precisely and only when they earn their place — never to impress.
- Avoids: "leverage" (as a verb), "solution," "innovative," "cutting-edge," "passionate," "exciting," "stakeholder," "synergy," "drive" (as in "drive outcomes"), "empower," "unlock," "deep dive."
- Avoids hedging language: "I think perhaps," "it could be argued that," "in some ways." States things directly and qualifies only when the qualification is meaningful.
- Uses "genuinely" and "actually" as emphasis — but only occasionally. These words signal that something matters more than the reader might assume.
- Comfortable with understated, slightly dry observations. Not joke-telling, but wry.

## Characteristic Patterns

1. **The pivot sentence.** Sets up what something is NOT, then says what it IS: "I'm not here to architect the most elegant system or chase the most ambitious scope. I'm here to help you build the right thing, the right way, and prove that it works."

2. **The honest aside.** Drops in a moment of candour mid-argument: "not because anything was wrong, but because I'd been thinking about a different way of working for some time."

3. **The grounded closer.** Ends sections with something concrete and human, not a grand statement: "Getting them right matters." / "Build useful software and sell it."

4. **Contrast as argument.** Juxtaposes two realities and lets the reader draw the conclusion: complex systems with questionable value vs. small tools people actually rely on.

5. **Specificity as credibility.** Doesn't say "various industries" — says "medical robotics, vapour sensing for defence systems, and multi-domain software consultancy at Cambridge Consultants."

## What NOT to Do

- Do not write in a way that sounds like marketing copy or consultancy brochure-speak.
- Do not use superlatives or hype: "world-class," "best-in-class," "unparalleled."
- Do not pad sentences to sound more substantial. If something can be said in 8 words, use 8 words.
- Do not apologise, over-explain, or caveat excessively. Omit rather than hedge.
- Do not use rhetorical questions as a persuasion device ("But what if there was a better way?").
- Do not write calls to action that sound desperate or salesy. Invitations, not urgency.
- Do not claim things that haven't been demonstrated. Credibility comes from what's been done, not from asserting quality.

## Calibrating for Context

- **Website copy:** Most polished version of the voice. Every sentence earns its place. Short paragraphs, clear structure, no filler.
- **LinkedIn posts:** Slightly more conversational. Can be a bit longer and more reflective. Still no fluff, but the pace is more relaxed.
- **Client emails:** Warm, direct, respectful of the reader's time. Gets to the point but isn't curt.
- **Technical writing:** Clear and precise. The voice recedes — the subject matter leads. But still avoids jargon for jargon's sake.
